# I Reviewed 200+ Fresher Resumes — Here Are the 7 Mistakes Almost Everyone Makes

*If you're a fresher struggling to land interviews, your resume is probably the problem. Not your skills.*

---

Last year, I helped screen resumes for campus placements at three engineering colleges. I went through over 200 resumes in a single week — for roles at companies like TCS, Infosys, Wipro, and a few startups.

The result was eye-opening.

**Nearly 80% of those resumes had the same avoidable mistakes.** Not formatting errors or typos (though there were plenty of those too). I'm talking about fundamental misunderstandings about what a resume is supposed to do.

The painful irony? Most of these candidates were smart, capable students. They had solid projects, decent grades, and genuine skills. But their resumes didn't reflect any of that.

If you're a fresher about to enter the job market — whether through campus placements, off-campus drives, or online applications — this article could save you months of frustration.

## Mistake #1: The "Everything Bagel" Resume

The most common mistake freshers make is trying to include *everything*. Father's name, date of birth, blood group, complete home address with pin code, hobbies like "listening to music and playing cricket," and a declaration signed at the bottom.

**Here's the thing:** Recruiters in 2025 don't need your father's occupation or your passport number. They need to understand — in 6 seconds — whether you're worth interviewing.

**What to include:**
- Name
- Email (professional, not coolguy2003@gmail.com)
- Phone number
- LinkedIn profile
- City (not full address)

That's it. Everything else is noise.

## Mistake #2: The Generic Objective Statement

> *"Seeking a challenging position in a reputed organization where I can utilize my skills and grow professionally."*

I saw some variation of this sentence on 150 out of 200 resumes. It says absolutely nothing. Every candidate wants a "challenging position." Every candidate wants to "grow professionally." This tells the recruiter zero about you specifically.

**Replace it with a Professional Summary:**

> *"B.Tech Computer Science graduate with hands-on experience in Java, Python, and React. Built a student management system handling 500+ records and completed a 3-month internship at [Company] working on REST API development. Seeking a Software Engineer role to apply full-stack development skills."*

See the difference? Specific. Concrete. Relevant.

## Mistake #3: Listing Duties Instead of Achievements

**Wrong:** "Responsible for frontend development during internship"

**Right:** "Developed responsive dashboard using React.js that reduced page load time by 35% and improved user engagement by 20%"

The first tells me what you were *supposed* to do. The second tells me what you actually *accomplished*. Recruiters care about impact, not responsibilities.

Even for college projects, frame them as achievements:
- "Built an e-commerce platform using MERN stack with payment integration, serving 50+ test users"
- "Created a machine learning model for sentiment analysis achieving 87% accuracy on 10,000+ reviews"

Numbers make your work real.

## Mistake #4: The Skills Section That Means Nothing

Here's a skills section I saw dozens of times:

> *MS Office, Internet, Communication Skills, Team Player, Hard Working, Quick Learner*

In 2025, listing MS Office and "Internet" as skills is like listing "can use a phone" — it's assumed. And soft skills like "team player" and "hard working" without evidence are just filler words.

**What a strong skills section looks like:**

> **Programming:** Java, Python, JavaScript, SQL
> **Frameworks:** React.js, Node.js, Spring Boot
> **Tools:** Git, Docker, Postman, VS Code
> **Databases:** MySQL, MongoDB
> **Cloud:** AWS (Basic), Firebase

Be specific. Be honest. Only list skills you can defend in an interview.

## Mistake #5: No Projects (or Poorly Described Ones)

As a fresher, your projects are your experience. They're the closest thing you have to professional work. Yet many resumes either skip projects entirely or describe them so vaguely they're meaningless.

**Bad project description:**
> "Online Shopping Website — Made a website for shopping using HTML and CSS"

**Good project description:**
> **E-Commerce Platform** | React, Node.js, MongoDB, Stripe
> - Built a full-stack e-commerce application with user authentication, product catalog, and cart functionality
> - Integrated Stripe payment gateway for secure transactions
> - Deployed on AWS EC2 with 99.9% uptime during testing phase

For each project, include:
1. **Technologies used** (this helps with ATS keyword matching)
2. **What you built** (specific functionality)
3. **Impact or metrics** (users, performance improvements, data handled)

## Mistake #6: Two Pages of Padding

A fresher's resume should be **one page**. Period.

If your resume is two pages, you're either including irrelevant information or formatting it poorly. Everything a fresher needs to communicate can fit on a single, well-organized page.

Common padding I see:
- Long list of unrelated hobbies
- "References available upon request" (nobody asks for this upfront)
- Detailed descriptions of college course curriculum
- A "Declaration" section at the bottom
- Repeating the same information in different sections

Cut ruthlessly. Every line on your resume should answer the question: "Does this help me get an interview?"

## Mistake #7: One Resume for Every Application

The biggest strategic mistake freshers make is creating a single resume and blasting it to every company.

A resume for TCS should emphasize different things than a resume for a startup. A data science role requires different keywords than a web development role. The job description is literally telling you what they want — your resume should reflect that.

At minimum, customize these for each application:
- **Professional summary** — align with the specific role
- **Skills order** — put the most relevant skills first
- **Project emphasis** — highlight projects related to the job

Yes, this takes more time per application. But 5 tailored applications will outperform 50 generic ones every single time.

## Your Fresher Resume Checklist

Before you submit your next application:

- [ ] One page, single-column layout
- [ ] Professional email address
- [ ] Specific professional summary (not a generic objective)
- [ ] 2-3 projects with technologies and measurable outcomes
- [ ] Technical skills relevant to the target role
- [ ] Education with CGPA (if above 6.0)
- [ ] Internship experience framed as achievements
- [ ] ATS-compatible formatting (no tables, graphics, or fancy fonts)
- [ ] Keywords matching the job description
- [ ] Zero spelling or grammatical errors
